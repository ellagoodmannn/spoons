About Projects

Unlike most designed objects, the spoon is a commonplace object that hasn't changed much since the Paleolithic era, 5,200 years ago. Complete re-imaginations of spoons are sparse, which I attribute to the spoon's expertise at serving its intended purpose — a vessel for foods that cannot be eaten by hand. When innovations are made, they almost always maintain the basic formal elements: the spoon and handle. Regarding new variations, the element that seems to radicalize the spoon the most is not the shape or spoon to handle ratio, but ornamentation and the number of instruments that connect to one handle — take the spork or spife as examples.

This project aims to question the seamless hand-to-mouth experience of using a vessel for eating. Why was the spoon standardized to be a shallow oval bowl with a flat long handle and how does the experience change without a rigid handle and bowl? How could spoons be developed for specific purposes again like during the Paleolithic era? Since the Middle Ages, why have spoons played such a major role in our lives, particularly at the beginning as infants, and at the end as elders?

I'd also like to acknowledge that this project is a formal investigation that could have been made about any object. I wanted to explore the spoon because not only is it the most primitive utensil, but its form is composed of two simple shapes — oval and rectangle (kind of). As nations and tech companies compete to create the most advanced artificial intelligence systems first, civilization is deeply content with the spoon.
